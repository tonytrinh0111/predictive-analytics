Time Series Data and Plots

For our exploration of time series, we will look at the Retail Sales (aggregate) time series of the US. Specifically we will focus on the beer, wine and liquor (BWL) category. The data is obtained from the St.Louis Federal Reserve (FRED) database.

The time series analytics tools that we use are from the forecast library. It is best to classify the data as a time series data type, as the modelling functions that we use recognize the characteristics of the time series through this definition.

library(forecast)
#Read in the data and classify it as a time series "ts" data type
#You can specify the start date and seasonal frequency of the data
bwl=ts(read.csv("RetailBWL.csv")[,2],start=c(1992,1),frequency=12)
#Plot the time series data and modify the title and label accordingly
plot(bwl,main="Retail Sales - Beer, Wine and Liqour",
     ylab="Millions of US Dollars")

Seasonal Adjustments

As was discussed in class, one of the considerations for dealing with time series is whether to seasonally adjust the data. If seasonal adjustment is the path you wish to take, the Seasonal, Trend by Loess (STL) decomposition method is a flexible way to separate trend, seasonal and error components.

#The stl() function for decomposition takes in two inputs
#s.window controls the smoothness of the seasonal component
#t.window controls the smoothness of the trend component
stl_bwl=stl(bwl,s.window=9,t.window=11)
#construct the seasonally adjusted series 
bwl_sadj=seasadj(stl_bwl)
plot(bwl_sadj,main="Seasonally Adjusted Retail Sales - Beer, Wine,Liqour",
     ylab="Millions of US Dollars")

The seasonally adjusted data reveals an acceleration in the trend towards the end of the sample that was not visible in the raw data plot.

#Plot the decomposition of the BWL data can also be useful
plot(stl_bwl)

#Plotting the trend of each seasonal component reveal a different angle
monthplot(stl_bwl)

Another feature that is more prominent is the change in seasonality over the sample period. The trough months are January and February, with the smallest amount of BWL sales relative to trend. Not surprisingly, the peak of the seasonality is in December, which coincide with the festive season of Christmas and the onset of the new year.

The monthplot() function provide a different perspective of the seasonality pattern. It gathers the movement of the seasonality, by month, across the years. We can see from this plot that the seasonality factor for the trough months (Jan and Feb) are decreasing over time, while the seasonality factor for the peak December month is increasing. These features contribute to the expanding magnitude of seasonal variations across the years.

Prediction Methods

Let us now develop the predictive models for this BWL time series. We will explore the long-horizon predictions first, and consider real-time predictions later on in the document.

First, we will split the data into the training and test set. For this exploration, the training set is between Jan 1992 and Dec 2010. The test set is between Jan 2011 to May 2020. We will also declare a function that computes the accuracy metrics of time series forecasts for repeated calculations later.

#Data split
bwl_train=window(bwl,end=c(2010,12))
bwl_test=window(bwl,start=c(2011,1))

#Function for accuracy metrics in time series data
tsaccuracy <- function (actual,predict) {
  stats<-matrix(0,1,5)
  colnames(stats)<-c("RMSE","MAE","MAPE","MASE","ACF")
  m=frequency(actual)
  actual=as.numeric(actual)
  dev<-actual-predict
  naive=c(rep(NA,m),actual[1:(length(actual)-m)])
  q<-dev/mean(abs(actual-naive),na.rm=TRUE)
  stats[1]=sqrt(mean(dev^2))
  stats[2]=mean(abs(dev))
  stats[3]=mean(100*abs(dev/actual))
  stats[4]=mean(abs(q))
  stats[5]=acf(dev,plot=FALSE)$acf[2]
  return(stats)
}

Time Series Regression

The first model we consider is the time series regression model.

\[y_t=b_0+b_1t+b_2S_{2t}+b_3S_{3t}+...+b_{12}S_{12t}+e_t\]

With the data characterised as a time series data type with frequency \(m=12\), we use the tslm() command to run a time series regression model. The advantage of this is that you can just specify that you want trend and seasonal components to be present, and the function will automatically construct the relevant trend and seasonal dummies. Remember that the function always leave the first season out as the reference.

To construct long-horizon predictions we use the forecast() function and pass in the regression model object. You also need to specify how far ahead you want to predict. In our case we predict \(h=113\) periods into the future. The plot() function takes in the forecast object and will plot the predictions along with their prediction intervals.

#Construct a summary
reg_bwl=tslm(bwl_train~trend+season)

One alternative of the regression model that might lead to an improvement is to consider modelling the time series in (natural) log transformation. By construction, the time series is now assumed to exhibit proportional growth, with the coefficient associated with the trend indicating the proportional growth per time period.

Exponential Smoothing

The exponential smoothing technique is data driven, and without parametric specifications for each component. It requires assumption on how the trend, seasonality and the error term are related (i.e.additive or multiplicative), but uses the data to drive the evolution of each component.

The long horizon forecast reveals the flaw of this method, which is that it often performs poorly over the long horizon prediction. The prediction flattens out over the test set, but the test set follows a strong positive trend.

ARIMA Predictions

The next class of model that can be used is the ARIMA model. The auto.arima() function selects the best fit model. Note that this function explores several alternative models using a logical algorithm, and it can take a while to run particularly if your data set is large.

Accuracy over long horizon predictions

The plots of the predictions depict the direction of the forecasts, and whether it tracks the test data. However, it does not give us a picture of which method is most accurate. Thus, we need to compute the accuracy metrics to assess the accuracy of the predictions over the long horizon.

stats_long=rbind(tsaccuracy(bwl_test,reg_fcast$mean),
                 tsaccuracy(bwl_test,exp(lreg_fcast$mean)),
                  tsaccuracy(bwl_test,ets_fcast$mean),
                  tsaccuracy(bwl_test,arima_fcast$mean))

The summary table indicates that the log regression method predict the best over long horizon for this particular data. In particular, all of the other three methods return MASE>1, indicating that using the most recent observed value would be more accurate than any of these methods. However, the log regression method returns MASE of 0.52, which means that it is an improvement on the naive prediction.

Real Time Predictions

Would the model ranking change if we are to consider real time predictions? That is, when a new data point becomes available, we update both our model and our predictions.

The design of the predictive assessment needs to be thought through carefully. Since the predictions are updated every time a new observation comes in, the process will be more computationally intensive. Unfortunately, there are no automatic functions that would do this in R, as we have to work through the logic of the prediction updating on a case by case basis. In the block of code below,we do the following:

  1. Create storage spaces for the predictions that we are going to generate over the test set. There will be one storage matrix for each model.
  2. Construct a for loop to:
    1. Update the model parameters at each time point
    2. Construct a one period ahead prediction (\(h=1\)) for each model
    3. Extract out the prediction and store it in the relevant matrix
    4. Expand the training set data for the following time point
  3. Plot the predictions against the observed data.
  4. Compute the accuracy metrics for each model.
#Storage spaces
ntrain=length(bwl_train)
ntest=length(bwl_test)
training=ts(bwl[1:ntrain],start=c(1992,1),frequency=12)
reg_yhat=matrix(0,ntest,1)
lreg_yhat=matrix(0,ntest,1)
ets_yhat=matrix(0,ntest,1)
arima_yhat=matrix(0,ntest,1)

##This loop is a real time updating loop
##It can take a little while to run
for(i in 1:ntest){
  #Estimate the models
  reg_bwl=tslm(training~trend+season)
  lreg_bwl=tslm(log(training)~trend+season)
  ets_bwl=ets(training,model="MAM",damped=TRUE)
  arima_bwl=Arima(training,order=c(2,1,0),seasonal=c(0,1,2))
  
  #Predict one period ahead
  reg_f=forecast(reg_bwl,h=1)
  lreg_f=forecast(lreg_bwl,h=1)
  ets_f=forecast(ets_bwl,h=1)
  arima_f=forecast(arima_bwl,h=1)
  
  #Save the predictions
  reg_yhat[i]=reg_f$mean[1]
  lreg_yhat[i]=exp(lreg_f$mean[1])
  ets_yhat[i]=ets_f$mean[1]
  arima_yhat[i]=arima_f$mean[1]
  
  #Expand the training set for the next iteration
  training=ts(bwl[1:(ntrain+i)],start=c(1992,1),frequency=12)
}

#Plot the forecasts
par(mfrow=c(2,2))
plot(bwl,col="gray",main="Real Time Predictions - Time Series Regression",
     ylab="Millions of US Dollars",cex.main=0.8)
lines(ts(reg_yhat,start=c(2011,1),frequency=12),col="blue")
plot(bwl,col="gray",main="Real Time Predictions - Time Series Log Regression",
     ylab="Millions of US Dollars",cex.main=0.8)
lines(ts(lreg_yhat,start=c(2011,1),frequency=12),col="blue")
plot(bwl,col="gray",main="Real Time Predictions - Exponential Smoothing",
     ylab="Millions of US Dollars",cex.main=0.8)
lines(ts(ets_yhat,start=c(2011,1),frequency=12),col="blue")
plot(bwl,col="gray",main="Real Time Predictions - ARIMA",
     ylab="Millions of US Dollars",cex.main=0.8)
lines(ts(arima_yhat,start=c(2011,1),frequency=12),col="blue")

#A summary of the real time predictive accuracy metrics
stats_short=rbind(tsaccuracy(bwl_test,reg_yhat),
                  tsaccuracy(bwl_test,lreg_yhat),
                  tsaccuracy(bwl_test,ets_yhat),
                  tsaccuracy(bwl_test,arima_yhat))
rownames(stats_short)=c("Regression", "Log Regression","Exp Smoothing", "ARIMA")

It should not be surprising that real time predictions are more accurate than long horizon predictions. This is evident from the lower levels of the accuracy metrics for real time prediction compared to those of the long horizon predictions across the board. Information adds value!

The relative ranking of the models are also different in the real time prediction, with the ARIMA model preferred according to the RMSE, MAE and MASE. The exponential smoothing method is preferred according to the MAPE and the autocorrelation of the prediction error. All models generate considerable improvements over the naive prediction, except for the linear regression model.

This difference in the model ranking highlights the fact that the predictive assessment process needs to be conducted according to the use of the predictive process. The user specific loss can also be brought into the predictive assessment process to aid the model selection for a particular contextual use.
